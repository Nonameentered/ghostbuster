Broca's and Wernicke's aphasia are two types of language disorders that have provided crucial insights into the localisation of language functions in the brain. They demonstrate that different aspects of language are processed in different neural regions. Broca's aphasia, also known as expressive aphasia, is caused by damage to Broca's area in the frontal lobe of the left hemisphere. It impairs the production of speech, as patients struggle to produce grammatical sentences and have a non-fluent, halting speech pattern. However, their comprehension of speech remains largely intact. This shows that Broca's area is critical for articulating speech, but not for understanding language. 

In contrast, Wernicke's aphasia arises from damage to Wernicke's area in the left temporal lobe. It is characterised by impaired comprehension of speech and poor speech production, but with a fluent speech pattern. Sufferers produce long and complex sentences that often lack meaning or contain nonsense words and phrases. This indicates that Wernicke's area is crucial for processing and understanding the meaning of language, while speech articulation depends on other brain regions.

The realisation that these two language functions are served by different brain areas was a milestone in neurolinguistics. The French surgeon Paul Broca first described his aphasia in 1861. He studied the brain of a patient who could only utter the syllable 'tan'. Post-mortem examination revealed a lesion in the left frontal lobe, hence known as Broca's area. Broca concluded that this region was responsible for speech production. The German neurologist Carl Wernicke later described impaired language comprehension accompanied by fluent jargon in patients with left temporal lobe damage. This supported the view that language functions are localised to different neural substrates.

Studies of Broca's and Wernicke's aphasia have since been complemented by modern brain imaging techniques like functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). These studies confirm that language processes like speech articulation, semantics, syntax are subserved by neighbouring yet distinct brain regions, predominantly in the left hemisphere. Damage to these regions through stroke, trauma or disease can selectively impair the specific language functions they support. The language profiles exhibited in Broca's and Wernicke's aphasia demonstrate this neural modular organisation in an exaggerated fashion. They provide an invaluable window into how language emerges from the dynamic interplay between distributed neural systems in the brain.
