Down's Syndrome, or Trisomy 21, is a genetic disorder caused by the presence of all or part of an extra 21st chromosome. It is one of the most common genetic causes of intellectual disability, occurring in 1 in 700 live births. Individuals with Down's Syndrome typically have characteristic facial features, physical characteristics, and intellectual delays. They often face significant health issues and developmental challenges throughout their lives. 

Down's Syndrome leads to a characteristic facial appearance, including upward slanting eyes, a flattened nasal bridge, small ears, and a protruding tongue. Individuals often have a single deep crease across the palm of the hand, called a simian crease, and shortened fingers. They tend to be shorter in stature and may have poor muscle tone or loose joints. Intellectual disability is common, ranging from mild to moderate impairment. Individuals have delayed language and motor development, often learning to walk at 15-22 months and speak in short sentences by age 6. There are several health issues frequently associated with Down's Syndrome. Congenital heart defects occur in nearly half of infants with Down's Syndrome. The most common defects are atrioventricular canal defects and ventricular septal defects, which often require surgery to correct. Recurrent ear infections and hearing loss are also common due to anatomical abnormalities. Obstructive sleep apnea, gastrointestinal blockages, and hypothyroidism can also occur. Individuals have an increased risk of leukemia, especially the acute megakaryocytic subtype of acute myeloid leukemia. They are also prone to respiratory infections, eye problems, and premature aging of the skin. 

The management of Down's Syndrome requires a multidisciplinary team of physicians, therapists, counselors, and care providers. Routine health maintenance includes more frequent well child visits, vaccinations, hearing and vision testing, and screening for heart defects and other medical issues. Early intervention programs with speech, physical, and occupational therapy should start as soon as possible after birth. Education and support for families are also crucial.

Overall, while Down's Syndrome is associated with intellectual disability and other health issues, with proper medical care and early intervention, individuals can live healthy and fulfilling lives. Many people with Down's Syndrome live independently as adults, hold jobs, and have relationships. With community support, people with Down's Syndrome can achieve their full potential and contribute to society in a meaningful way.
