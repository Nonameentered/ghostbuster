The objective of an acid-base titration is to determine the concentration of an unknown acid or base by neutralizing it with a standardized solution of a base or acid of known concentration. Titration is commonly used to measure the total acid concentration in vinegar, which contains acetic acid (CH3COOH). By titrating a vinegar sample with a standardized solution of sodium hydroxide (NaOH), the concentration of acetic acid can be determined.

The acetic acid is neutralized by the sodium hydroxide, producing sodium acetate and water. The endpoint of the titration is detected using an indicator such as phenolphthalein, which is colorless in acidic solutions but turns pink in basic solutions. As NaOH is added from a burette and mixed into the vinegar sample, the solution remains colorless until neutralization. The endpoint is reached once the solution turns permanently pink, indicating excess NaOH has been added.

To prepare the NaOH titrant, a solution is first standardized to determine its exact concentration. This is done by titrating a known mass of potassium hydrogen phthalate (KHC8H4O4) with the NaOH solution. KHC8H4O4 is an acid with a known number of moles, so by calculating the moles of KHC8H4O4 neutralized, the molarity of the NaOH solution can be determined. 

pH paper and barium chloride/silver nitrate solutions are used to test properties of the vinegar samples. pH paper measures the pH to ensure the vinegar is acidic before the titration. Barium chloride forms a precipitate with sulfate ions, so no change indicates the absence of sulfates in the vinegar. Silver nitrate solution forms a precipitate with chloride ions; no change indicates the absence of chlorides. These tests ensure only acetic acid is being measured in the titration.  

To calculate the % w/v acetic acid, the volume and molarity of the NaOH titrant used to neutralize the sample must be known, as well as the volume of vinegar titrated. For example, if 25 mL of 1.5 M NaOH is needed to neutralize 15 mL of vinegar, the calculations would be: 
moles NaOH = M x V = 1.5 mol/L x 0.025 L = 0.0375 mol
Since NaOH and acetic acid neutralize in a 1:1 ratio:
moles CH3COOH = 0.0375 mol
 mass CH3COOH = moles x molecular weight = 0.0375 mol x 60 g/mol = 2.25 g
% w/v = (mass solute / volume solution) x 100% = (2.25 g / 0.015 L) x 100% = 15%

An indicator detects the endpoint, and various tests help ensure only acetic acid is measured. By calculating the moles of NaOH needed to neutralize the sample, the mass and concentration of acetic acid in the vinegar can be determined.
