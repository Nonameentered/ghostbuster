Data encryption is the process of encoding data in such a way that only authorized parties can read it. It is achieved by using encryption algorithms to convert the original data, known as plaintext, into incomprehensible data, known as ciphertext. Decryption algorithms are required to convert the ciphertext back to plaintext. Encryption is used to protect data and communications in insecure environments like the public Internet.

There are two main types of encryption algorithms: block ciphers and stream ciphers. Block ciphers encrypt data in blocks of a fixed size, padding the final block if needed. Popular block ciphers include AES and DES. Stream ciphers encrypt data bit-by-bit or byte-by-byte. They generate a keystream that is XORed with the plaintext to produce the ciphertext. Common stream ciphers include RC4 and Salsa20.

Block ciphers are generally considered more resistant to attack due to their diffusion properties that spread cipher building interactions to multiple block locations. However, they have lower throughput since they reset their internal state for each block. Stream ciphers have higher throughput but are more prone to attacks if the same keystream is used repeatedly. As a result, block ciphers are more suitable for data-at-rest encryption like disk encryption while stream ciphers are better suited for data-in-transit encryption in communications.

There are several challenges in implementing and comparing encryption algorithms. Firstly, encryption algorithms can be very complex to implement efficiently while avoiding vulnerabilities. They require expertise in cryptography, software engineering and secure coding practices. Secondly, comparing algorithms objectively is difficult due to the variety of criteria involved including speed, security level, standardization level, cost of implementation, and application requirements. These criteria often conflict and a trade-off analysis is needed to select an algorithm for a specific application and environment.
