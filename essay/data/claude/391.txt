Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) refers to infection and inflammation of the female reproductive organs such as the fallopian tubes, ovaries, and uterus. The most common symptoms of PID include lower abdominal pain, abnormal vaginal discharge that may have a foul odor, irregular menstrual bleeding, pain during intercourse, and fever. On pelvic exam, the physician may detect uterine, adnexal, or cervical motion tenderness. PID is most often caused by sexually transmitted infections (STIs) such as chlamydia and gonorrhea that ascend from the lower genital tract. Other risk factors for PID include multiple sexual partners, a history of STIs, and poor access to reproductive health care.  

The mainstays of treatment for PID include antibiotics to cover common genital tract pathogens like Neisseria gonorrhoeae and Chlamydia trachomatis, as well as anaerobic bacteria and gram-negative rods. Hospitalization and intravenous antibiotics are recommended for severe PID. In some cases, especially if an abscess is present, surgical drainage or removal of the fallopian tubes (salpingectomy) may be required. Early diagnosis and treatment of PID is critical to minimizing long-term consequences like infertility and chronic pelvic pain. 

Chronic pelvic pain lasting more than 6 months affects up to 24% of women at some point. It can persist even after treatment of PID due to permanent damage and scarring. Management of chronic pelvic pain requires a multidisciplinary approach including pain medications, hormonal treatments, trigger point injections, lifestyle changes, and counseling. Psychological implications of infertility and chronic pain include depression, anxiety, reduced libido and self-esteem. The financial costs of diagnosis and treatment also substantially impact the patient and the health care system.

In summary, PID is a common condition that can lead to serious long-term complications if left untreated. With the increasing rates of STIs in young, sexually active women, clinicians must have a high index of suspicion for PID. Early diagnosis, appropriate antibiotic therapy, and an integrated approach to managing chronic pelvic pain are essential to improving outcomes and quality of life in women with PID.
