Transient Ischaemic Attacks (TIAs) are brief episodes of neurological dysfunction resulting from focal cerebral ischaemia that last typically less than 24 hours. They are caused by temporary interruptions in blood flow, often due to emboli or thrombi. Patients may experience symptoms such as weakness, numbness, speech difficulty, vision problems or dizziness. Examination may reveal focal neurological signs that resolve. 

The main sources of emboli to the brain are the heart (e.g. atrial fibrillation) and atherosclerotic plaques in the carotid arteries. Differential diagnoses for transient episodes include migraine, seizures, hypoglycaemia and brain tumours. Carotid artery stenosis can be worsened by chronic lung and heart disease due to increased blood viscosity and turbulence.

Carotid endarterectomy has been shown to reduce stroke risk in symptomatic patients with significant carotid stenosis. It is often considered over best medical therapy alone which relies on antiplatelet drugs and control of vascular risk factors. Surgery within 2 weeks of last symptoms offers greatest benefit. In asymptomatic stenosis, surgery is only considered if there is a high grade stenosis (70-99%) and life expectancy of 5-10 years.

The major risk factors for carotid artery disease are age, hypertension, smoking, hypercholesterolaemia, diabetes and atherosclerosis. Lifestyle changes and medical management of risk factors can help prevent and slow disease progression. Ethical issues may arise regarding non-resuscitation requests, in which case risks and benefits of surgery and patient wishes must be considered.

Transcranial Doppler ultrasound uses sound waves to detect microembolic signals which indicate ulceration of plaques and risk of future stroke. This can be used to monitor patients and guide management. 

In summary, TIAs result from temporary lack of blood flow to parts of the brain. The main causes are emboli from the heart or carotid arteries. Examination may show temporary neurological signs. Carotid endarterectomy can reduce stroke risk, especially if performed early after symptoms. However, medical management of risk factors and lifestyle changes remain important for prevention and progression of disease. TCD ultrasound aids monitoring. Ethical issues must be considered for each patient based on individual situation and wishes.
