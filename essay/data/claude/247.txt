The Immunoglobulin G (IgG) is the most abundant antibody found in the human immune system. It plays a critical role in the immune response by binding to foreign antigens and marking them for destruction. The basic unit of an IgG molecule is composed of four polypeptide chains - two identical heavy chains and two identical light chains. The heavy and light chains are held together by disulphide bonds to form a 'Y' shaped structure. 

The heavy chains of IgG contain four immunoglobulin domains, while the light chains contain two immunoglobulin domains. An immunoglobulin domain is a protein domain of about 110 amino acids that adopts an immunoglobulin fold - a sandwich of two beta sheets with a greek key topology. The immunoglobulin domains are important for the structure and function of IgG as they contain amino acids that are crucial for antigen binding and Fc receptor interactions.

The two arms of the Y-shaped IgG molecule contain the variable regions, known as the Fab fragments, while the stem of the Y contains the constant Fc region. The variable regions differ between individual antibodies and contain the antigen binding sites. They are composed of hypervariable complementarity determining regions (CDRs) on a framework of more conserved immunoglobulin domains. The diversity of the variable regions gives rise to the five different classes of immunoglobulins with different biological functions. IgG itself has four subclasses.

The Fc region is so named because it binds to various Fc receptors on cells in the immune system. It is composed of two heavy chain immunoglobulin domains and is involved in activation of complement proteins and binding to Fc receptors on macrophages, neutrophils and dendritic cells. The Fc region remains largely constant for each antibody class and subclass.

The immunoglobulin gene rearrangement process achieves diversity of the variable regions through a random recombination of different gene segments - variable (V), diversity (D) and joining (J) segments. Somatic hypermutation and affinity maturation further increase variation. This allows for an immense number of potential combinations to produce antibodies against virtually any antigen.

When an IgG antibody binds to an antigen, it forms an immunglobulin-antigen complex. This activates secondary immune responses such as phagocytosis, complement activation and antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC). The strength of antigen binding depends on the affinity and avidity of an antibody which in turn depends on the complementarity determining regions and immunoglobulin fold of the variable regions.

In summary, the Immunoglobulin G molecule possesses a conserved domain structure across all antibodies but achieves remarkable diversity through gene rearrangement and variation in the variable regions. It plays a vital role in defending against pathogens through its ability to form antibody-antigen complexes and trigger secondary immune responses. Constant improvement of affinity and avidity allows the immune system to produce highly specific antibodies against any foreign antigen.
