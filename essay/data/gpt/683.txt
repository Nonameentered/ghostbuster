Small bowel obstruction occurs when the normal flow of intestinal contents is impeded, which leads to distention of the proximal part of the intestine and accumulation of fluid and gas. The causes of SBO can be broadly classified into two categories: mechanical obstruction and functional obstruction. Mechanical obstruction is caused by physical blockages that prevent the intestinal contents from flowing through the lumen. This can be caused by a variety of factors, including adhesions, hernias, tumors, strictures, and volvulus. Functional obstruction, on the other hand, is caused by impaired motility of the intestinal wall, which leads to the accumulation of fluid and gas and subsequent distension of the intestine. This can be caused by a variety of factors, including ileus, drugs, metabolic disorders, and neurogenic disorders.
The symptoms of SBO can vary depending on the severity and location of the obstruction. Patients with SBO typically complain of abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. They may also experience bloating, constipation, diarrhea, and dehydration. In severe cases, the patient may develop signs of sepsis, including fever, tachycardia, and hypotension. In rare cases, the patient may experience ischemia, perforation, and intestinal necrosis, which requires urgent surgical intervention.
The diagnosis of SBO can be made by a variety of methods, including physical examination, laboratory tests, imaging studies, and endoscopic examinations. In most cases, a combination of these methods is used to make an accurate diagnosis. Physical examination may reveal distention of the abdomen, tenderness to palpation, and abnormal bowel sounds. Laboratory tests may reveal electrolyte imbalances, metabolic acidosis, and elevated white blood cell counts. Imaging studies, including plain radiography, ultrasound, computed tomography (CT), and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), can help identify the location and nature of the obstruction. Endoscopic examinations, including colonoscopy and upper endoscopy, may be useful in select cases.
The management of SBO depends on the severity and underlying cause of the obstruction. In mild cases, conservative management may be appropriate, which involves bowel rest, nasogastric decompression, intravenous fluids, and electrolyte replacement. In more severe cases, surgery may be necessary to relieve the obstruction. There are several options for surgical management, including adhesiolysis, bowel resection, and hernia repair. The choice of surgical management depends on the etiology and severity of the obstruction.
