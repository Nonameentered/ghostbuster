Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL) is a type of cancer that affects the white blood cells, specifically the lymphocytes. It is the most common form of leukaemia found in adults, accounting for a third of all leukaemia cases. CLL develops when the body produces too many immature lymphocytes, which do not function properly and may result in an impaired immune system. The cancerous cells accumulate in the bone marrow, blood, and lymph nodes, disrupting the normal functions of the body.
Despite CLL being prevalent, its causes remain unknown. Age, genetic predisposition, and exposure to certain environmental factors may increase the risk of developing CLL. Symptoms of CLL develop slowly, and some people can have the disease without any symptoms at all. The most frequent symptoms are swollen lymph nodes, fatigue, weight loss, and fever and infections.
To diagnose CLL, a detailed medical history is taken, followed by a physical exam and laboratory tests. The blood tests performed include a complete blood count, which evaluates the number of white and red blood cells and platelets. In addition, flow cytometry, a test that measures the number, type, and size of cells, is used to diagnose CLL. Furthermore, a bone marrow biopsy will be performed to analyze the bone marrow and check for the presence of cancerous cells.
Depending on the stage and severity of the disease, various treatment options are available for CLL. The type of treatment selected will depend on the patient's age and general health, the severity of the disease, and the stage of the cancer. CLL is typically considered to be an indolent or slow-growing cancer that does not require treatment at an early stage; however, treatment should be started if the cancer becomes aggressive and spreads rapidly. The most common CLL treatment options include chemotherapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, bone marrow transplantation, and radiation therapy.
Chemotherapy is the most widely used treatment for CLL. It involves the use of drugs such as fludarabine and chlorambucil to destroy cancerous cells and slow the disease's progression. Chemotherapy is usually administered orally or through an intravenous infusion. It can cause side effects such as nausea, hair loss, and a weakened immune system.
Immunotherapy is another treatment option for CLL. Immunotherapy works by stimulating the body's immune system to attack cancer cells. Patients receive a synthetic protein called a monoclonal antibody that binds to a specific protein on the surface of cancer cells, aiding the immune system in eliminating cancerous cells. Some side effects of immunotherapy may include vomiting, fatigue, and diarrhea.
Targeted therapy is another type of CLL treatment. Targeted therapies are drugs that inhibit the activity of specific proteins that are involved in the cancerous cell's growth and spread. They work by targeting specific proteins involved in the cancerous cell's growth and spreading. Targeted therapy may cause side effects such as diarrhea, skin rash, and fatigue.
Bone marrow transplant is another option for CLL treatment. This procedure involves the replacement of bone marrow with healthy cells from a suitable donor. The goal of the transplant is to eradicate any remaining cancer cells, as well as replacing damaged or destroyed immune cells. The side effects of a transplant can include nausea, fatigue, and infection.
Radiation therapy can be used in certain cases for CLL treatment, primarily if the disease spreads to the lymph nodes or spleen. Radiation therapy involves the use of high-energy radiation to destroy cancerous cells in the body.

The disease can develop slowly, and symptoms may manifest over time, such as swollen lymph nodes, fatigue, and weight loss. Diagnosis is achieved through medical history, physical examination, and laboratory tests. The treatment selected depends on the age and general health of the patient, the severity of the disease, and the stage of the cancer. The most common CLL treatment options include chemotherapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, bone marrow transplantation, and radiation therapy. The earlier the cancer is detected, the easier it is to treat and manage, so early detection and diagnosis are essential.
