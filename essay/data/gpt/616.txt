Investigating the rate of a chemical reaction is critical in understanding the reaction's kinetics and designing efficient industrial processes that utilize the reaction. The goal of an investigation into the rate of a chemical reaction is to establish the relationship between the rate of reaction and the reactants' concentrations. The investigation aims to determine how the reaction's kinetics change as the reaction progresses and how factors such as temperature affect the rate of reaction. The following essay aims to explain the goals and methods of investigating the rate of a chemical reaction and how temperature affects this process.
Chemical kinetics involves studying the rate of a chemical reaction, which is the measure of how fast the reactants are consumed or how fast the products are formed. The rate of a reaction can vary based on the reactants' concentration, temperature, and other environmental factors. Therefore, the investigation of a reaction's rate involves varying one parameter while keeping the others constant to determine its effect on the reaction rate.
To investigate the rate of a reaction, researchers generally measure the concentration of the reactants and products over time using a spectrophotometer or other analytical methods. In doing so, a graph is plotted with the concentration of reactants and products over time. The curve obtained from this graph is known as the progress curve and gives an idea of how fast the reaction is proceeding.
The reaction rate is determined by measuring the change in reactant concentration with time and plotting it against time. As the reaction progresses, the reactant concentration decreases, while the product concentration increases. Hence, the slope of the graph decreases with time, indicating that the reaction rate is decreasing. The initial slope of the graph, known as the initial rate, is used to determine the reaction rate at the start of the reaction.
Several factors can affect the rate of a reaction, one of which is temperature. Temperature affects the rate of a reaction by changing the kinetic energy of the reactants. When the temperature is increased, the kinetic energy of the reactants increases, and they move faster, leading to more frequent collisions between the reactants and a higher probability of successful collisions. The Arrhenius equation can be used to estimate the effect of temperature on the rate of a reaction.

Rate = k[benzyl alcohol][permanganate ion][H+]
In this equation, k is the rate constant, and benzyl alcohol, permanganate ion, and H+ are the reactants' concentrations.

The reaction's activation energy and pre-exponential factor can be calculated using the Arrhenius equation, which is given below:
k = A exp(-Ea/RT)
In this equation, k is the rate constant, A is the pre-exponential factor, Ea is the activation energy, R is the gas constant, and T is the absolute temperature.
To calculate the activation energy and pre-exponential factor, experimental data is collected by varying the temperature and measuring the reaction rate at each temperature. The collected data is then plotted on a graph, with the y-axis showing the natural logarithm of the rate constant, and the x-axis showing the reciprocal of the absolute temperature. The activation energy can be determined from the slope of the graph, while the pre-exponential factor can be calculated from the y-intercept.
There are limitations to using the Arrhenius equation to calculate the activation energy and pre-exponential factor. For instance, the equation assumes that the reaction follows a single-step mechanism and that the reactants have collided with enough frequency and energy. Additionally, it assumes that the temperature and pressure are constant, which might not be valid in all cases.
