Finding the root of a function is a fundamental concept in mathematics, science, and engineering. It involves finding the value of an input variable that makes the output of a function equal to zero. There are several techniques to find the root of a function, but three of the most effective methods are the bisection method, the secant method, and the Newton-Raphson method. Each technique has its strengths and limitations, and the selection of the appropriate method depends on the problem's complexity and the desired accuracy level.
The bisection method is a simple and robust numerical algorithm that uses a repeated halving of an interval to converge to a root. The method works by selecting two initial values, a and b, such that f(a) and f(b) have opposite signs. Then, the midpoint c = (a+b)/2 is calculated, and the sign of f(c) is checked. If f(c) has the same sign as f(a), the new interval becomes [c,b], otherwise, the interval becomes [a,c]. This process is repeated until the interval becomes smaller than a specified tolerance level.

One of the advantages of the bisection method is its ability to find multiple roots of a function. For example, consider the function f(x) = x^3 – x – 1, which has three roots at approximately -1.3247, 0.6180, and 1.3067. In Figure 1, the bisection method converged to the root at approximately 1.32 after ten iterations, with an accuracy of 10^-3. However, the bisection method is relatively slow and may require a large number of iterations to achieve a high accuracy level.
The secant method is another iterative technique for finding the root of a function. The method uses the slope of a secant line to approximate the root of the function. Suppose we have two initial values, x0 and x1, such that f(x0) and f(x1) have opposite signs. Then, we can calculate the next approximations, x2, x3, and so on, using the formula:
xi+1 = xi – f(xi) (xi – xi-1) / (f(xi) – f(xi-1))
The secant method is faster than the bisection method, but its convergence rate is only approximately linear. This means that it may take more iterations to achieve a high accuracy level compared to some more advanced numerical methods.

In Figure 2, we can see the convergence of the secant method for the same function as before, f(x) = x^3 – x – 1, with an accuracy level of 10^-3. The secant method converged to a root at approximately 1.32 after six iterations, which is faster than the bisection method.
The Newton-Raphson method, also known as the Newton method, is a commonly used numerical technique for finding the root of a function. The method uses a sequence of tangent lines to iteratively approach a root. Given an initial guess, x0, we can find the next approximations, xn, using the formula:
xn+1 = xn – f(xn) / f’(xn)
where f’(xn) is the derivative of f(xn) evaluated at xn. The Newton-Raphson method is known for its fast convergence rate, which is quadratic. This means that the number of significant digits approximately doubles in each iteration.

In Figure 3, we can see the convergence of the Newton-Raphson method for the same function, f(x) = x^3 – x – 1, with an accuracy level of 10^-3. The method converged to a root at approximately 1.32 after only three iterations, which is significantly faster than the bisection and secant methods.
The Newton-Raphson method is considered the most accurate method due to its fast convergence rate and high accuracy level. The method's accuracy depends on the initial guess of the root, the smoothness of the function, and the behavior of the derivative at the root. If the initial guess is close to the root, the method converges quickly, resulting in high accuracy. However, if the derivative is small at some points, the convergence of the Newton-Raphson method can slow down or fail to converge.
In conclusion, the bisection method, the secant method, and the Newton-Raphson method are some of the effective methods for finding the root of a function. The selection of the appropriate method depends on the nature of the function, the required accuracy level, and the availability of the derivative. Although the bisection and secant methods are relatively simple and robust, the Newton-Raphson method is considered the most accurate due to its fast convergence rate and high accuracy level. However, the Newton-Raphson method requires the function's derivative, which may not always be available for some functions. Therefore, it is important to evaluate the pros and cons of each method before applying it to a particular function.
