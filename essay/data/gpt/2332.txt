SDS-PAGE, or Sodium Dodecyl Sulfate Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis, is a common laboratory technique used to separate and analyze proteins. One crucial component of this technique is the reducing agent. A reducing agent is a substance that donates electrons and reduces the disulfide bonds that hold together the protein subunits. The reducing agent in SDS-PAGE is usually beta-mercaptoethanol or dithiothreitol.
The role of the reducing agent in SDS-PAGE is essential because disulfide bonds hold protein subunits together, and without reducing agents breaking these bonds, the protein cannot be separated. SDS-PAGE works under denaturing conditions, which means that the protein molecules are unfolded and coated with negatively charged SDS molecules. By using the reducing agent to break the disulfide bonds, each protein is then linearized, becoming negative and separated by the size of their polypeptide chains.
The relationship between molecular weight and protein band migration distance in SDS-PAGE follows a linear correlation. A standard curve is created by running known proteins of different molecular weights alongside the unknown proteins, and the band migration distance is then measured. Unknown proteins 1 and 2 can then be identified by comparing their band migration distance with the standard curve. The standard curve is a graphical representation of the migration distance vs. molecular weight of known proteins. The slope of the line represents the migration of distance per logarithm of molecular weight. The reduced density of the SDS-PAGE gel during the separation process spreads out each unknown protein according to its molecular weight. Therefore, the larger the molecular weight, the less distance each protein will travel and will correspond to the upper band of the ladder on the gel.
Different proteins play specific roles in the body. Triose-phosphate isomerase, lactate dehydrogenase, ovalbumin, pyruvate kinase, fructose-6-phosphate kinase, and beta-galactosidase, are vital enzymes found in different organisms. Triose-phosphate isomerase is responsible for glycolysis, and without this enzyme, ATP cannot be produced from glucose in cells. Lactate dehydrogenase, on the other hand, is responsible for creating lactic acid from pyruvate in the absence of sufficient oxygen. Ovalbumin is a protein found in egg whites that serves as a source of amino acids for embryonic development. Pyruvate kinase is an enzyme essential for energy conversion and plays a crucial role in the production of ATP. It converts phosphoenolpyruvate to pyruvate. Fructose-6-phosphate kinase is an enzyme that catalyzes the conversion of fructose-6-phosphate to fructose 1,6 bisphosphate. Finally, beta-galactosidase is an enzyme that hydrolyzes lactose into glucose and galactose.
