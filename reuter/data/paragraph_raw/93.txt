In a landmark decision, the United States Supreme Court has ruled that the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) lacks the authority to regulate most foreign currency transactions conducted outside US borders.
The decision, which was handed down on Monday, marks a significant shift in how the CFTC is able to enforce commodities trading rules, and could have far-reaching implications for how foreign currency trades are conducted around the world.
The case, Manhattan Regional Agency LLC v. United States Commodity Futures Trading Commission, was brought by a New York-based financial services company that had been accused by the CFTC of illegally trading in foreign currencies. The company, which argued that the CFTC had overstepped its authority, challenged the agency's jurisdiction, prompting the Supreme Court to take up the case. The court ultimately decided the case on two questions: does the CFTC have the jurisdiction to regulate foreign currency transactions; and if so, was the CFTC's enforcement of the law in this case appropriate?
The first question was a matter of interpretation of existing laws, while the second was more specific to the case at hand.
In a 7-2 decision, the Supreme Court ruled that the CFTC does not have the authority to regulate most foreign currency transactions that take place outside the US, except in cases where there is a direct connection to US markets or participants.
The decision was based on a narrow interpretation of the Commodity Exchange Act, which gives the CFTC regulatory authority over commodities trading, but which does not specifically mention foreign currency transactions.
Writing for the majority, Justice Brett Kavanaugh noted that the Commodity Futures Trading Commission could enforce its authority in cases where there was a "direct and significant connection" between the transaction and US markets or participants. But he added that in most cases, the CFTC lacked the authority to police foreign currency trades conducted outside the US.
"Our conclusion is narrow," Kavanaugh wrote. "We do not hold that all foreign currency transactions are beyond the CFTC's reach, only that those transactions where there is no direct and significant connection to US markets are not."
The ruling was a blow to the CFTC, which had argued that it needed the authority to regulate foreign currency transactions in order to protect investors and maintain market stability.
In a statement responding to the decision, CFTC Chairman Heath Tarbert acknowledged the ruling and said the agency would work with Congress and international regulators to ensure the integrity of global markets.
"While we are disappointed in the Supreme Court's decision, we respect its authority and will work with Congress and our partners across the globe to ensure that we continue to ensure that international regulatory oversight of our global markets remains effective," Tarbert said.

CFTC officials argued that the platform was subject to its jurisdiction because the transactions had the potential to impact US markets and US participants. They also contended that the company had targeted customers based in the US, and that therefore it should be subject to CFTC authority.
The Supreme Court agreed with NYPC, however, ruling that the company's platform was exempt from CFTC jurisdiction because it operated outside the US.
The ruling has been widely praised by those who argue that the CFTC should not have the authority to regulate foreign currency transactions conducted outside US borders. Critics of the CFTC have argued that the agency is overreaching in its jurisdiction and should not be able to regulate activities that have no direct effect on US markets. The Supreme Court's decision supports this view, and could open the door to more foreign currency transactions taking place outside US jurisdiction.
But others have expressed concern that the decision could make it harder for regulators to police the commodities market, particularly in cases where there may be cross-border transactions or where market manipulation is suspected.

"Without regulatory authority over foreign transactions, market participants and investors may be more vulnerable to manipulative practices and other forms of financial crime," wrote Justice Breyer.
