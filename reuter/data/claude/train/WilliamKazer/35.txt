China Bans Poultry and Products from Missouri and Oklahoma Due to Outbreak of Deadly Disease 

China announced today that it is banning all poultry and poultry products from the states of Missouri and Oklahoma to prevent the spread of a highly pathogenic strain of avian influenza that has been detected at several poultry farms. The sweeping ban includes live birds as well as meat, eggs, and feathers.

According to China's Ministry of Agriculture, the ban is in response to reports from the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) that an H5N2 avian flu virus has been found in poultry flocks in both Missouri and Oklahoma. The virus has been detected at several locations in each state over the past month. To date, over 100,000 turkeys and chickens have been euthanized to curb the spread of the disease.

The USDA believes the virus was initially transported into the region by wild birds migrating through the area. Avian influenza viruses can spread easily between birds and some of these viruses have the potential to mutate into a form that is more easily transmitted between humans, raising concerns of a broader epidemic.

China is the world's largest consumer of poultry products, importing over $1 billion in poultry meat and over $500 million in live birds from the U.S. annually. "We take the threat from avian influenza very seriously and will take all necessary measures to prevent entry of this virus into China," said a Ministry of Agriculture spokesman. The ban will remain in place until China is satisfied that the outbreak in Missouri and Oklahoma has been fully contained.

The economic losses from this ban are expected to be substantial for Missouri and Oklahoma poultry farmers who rely heavily on export markets. The poultry industry contributes over $2 billion to Missouri's economy and $3.7 billion to Oklahoma's economy each year. Leaders from both states have pledged to work closely with USDA officials to curb the outbreak as quickly as possible and limit the damage.
